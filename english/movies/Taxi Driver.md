## Urban Alienation Turned Into Violence

*Taxi Driver* remains one of the defining portraits of loneliness in American cinema. Martin Scorsese and Paul Schrader create a city that feels feverish, polluted, and morally dislocated, then place Travis Bickle inside it as both symptom and witness. The film does not excuse his instability, but it does insist on its social atmosphere.

Robert De Niro's performance is central to that achievement. Travis is not presented as a simple antihero or monster. He is fractured, needy, obsessive, and increasingly dangerous, which makes the film's drift toward violence feel disturbingly plausible.

## Why the Film Still Feels Dangerous

Its power comes from subjectivity. The city is seen through Travis's insomnia, resentment, and fantasy of purification, so viewers are forced close to a consciousness they cannot trust. That closeness gives the film its unsettling force.

*Taxi Driver* endures because it understands that isolation can mutate into ideology. It is both a character study and a warning about the seductive logic of self-appointed salvation.

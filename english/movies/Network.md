## Rage Turned Into Product

*Network* remains frightening because it sees how media institutions can convert public feeling into merchandise. On paper, its premise sounds satirical: an unstable news anchor becomes ratings gold, executives embrace spectacle, and outrage is packaged as entertainment. In practice, the film now feels less like exaggeration than diagnosis. Paddy Chayefsky's script is loud, theatrical, and intentionally excessive because it understands that a culture of performance may require overstatement to be described accurately.

Sidney Lumet keeps the film from floating into abstraction by grounding it in institutions, offices, meetings, and ambitions. Peter Finch gives Howard Beale prophetic mania, Faye Dunaway plays corporate calculation with terrifying clarity, and William Holden supplies the moral exhaustion of someone watching a profession dissolve into programming.

## Why It Keeps Getting More Accurate

The film's predictive force has only grown. Its central insight is not just that television can be cynical, but that systems built on attention will eventually absorb sincerity, anger, and crisis as content. Once that process starts, truth matters less than whether emotion can be made profitable.

That is why *Network* still feels contemporary. It is not nostalgic for a pure journalistic past. It understands the conflict between commerce and public responsibility as internal to the medium itself. The film endures because it treats media corruption not as an accident, but as a structural temptation.

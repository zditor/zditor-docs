## Desire Turned Into Obsession

*Vertigo* is one of Hitchcock's most unsettling films because it is not driven by mystery alone. Beneath the detective structure lies a study of fixation, projection, and the violence hidden inside romantic idealization. What begins as fascination gradually reveals itself as control.

James Stewart's performance is crucial to that effect. He brings enough vulnerability to Scottie that the character never feels merely predatory, yet the film refuses to excuse the damage his obsession creates. That tension gives the film its enduring psychological charge.

## Why the Film Grows More Disturbing Over Time

The color design, camera movement, and dreamlike repetitions turn San Francisco into a landscape of memory and hallucination. Hitchcock builds not just suspense, but a world in which identity itself becomes unstable. Kim Novak's dual presence intensifies that instability.

*Vertigo* endures because it sees love corrupted by fantasy. Its tragedy is not simply that Scottie loses the woman he wants, but that he cannot love without remaking the object of desire into an image.

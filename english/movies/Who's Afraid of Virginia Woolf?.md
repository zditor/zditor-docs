## Marriage as Performance and Combat

*Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?* remains ferocious because it turns conversation into combat without reducing emotional damage to mere theatrical display. Mike Nichols preserves the claustrophobic pressure of Edward Albee's writing while making the screen space feel increasingly unstable and exposed.

Elizabeth Taylor and Richard Burton give performances of extraordinary volatility. Their insults, games, confessions, and manipulations are exhausting by design, because the film is interested in how intimacy can become a system of mutual destruction.

## Why the Film Still Feels Raw

Its power comes from the refusal to provide polite distance. The audience is forced to stay with humiliation, fantasy, dependency, and cruelty long enough for the emotional structures underneath them to become visible.

*Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?* endures because it understands that love can survive inside damage without ceasing to be damaging.
